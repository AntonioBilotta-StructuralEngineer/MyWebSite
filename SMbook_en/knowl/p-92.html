The application of the right polar decomposition to (1.3.9) furnishes

\begin{equation} d\vec{x} = \tens{F}\, d\vec{X} = \tens{R}\, \left(\tens{U}\, d\vec{X}\right) \,,\tag{1.6.5} \end{equation}

which shows how the vector \(d\vec{X}\) is first stretched and after rotated. By applying the left polar decomposition it is obtained

\begin{equation} d\vec{x} = \tens{F}\, d\vec{X} = \tens{V}\, \left(\tens{R}\, d\vec{X}\right) \,,\tag{1.6.6} \end{equation}

to be interpreted as a deformation sequence of \(d\vec{X}\) followed by a rotation. What has been said is illustrated by the following figure.

In particular, we illustrate the case in which the right stretch tensor, \(\tens{U} \text{,}\) produces only an elongation in the horizontal direction and, subsequently, the tensor \(\tens{R} \) produces a rotation of 45 \(^o\text{.}\) It is evident that if the rotation is applied first, second and third transformation sequence in the figure, the stretch tensor cannot be the same but the left tensor must be different and equal to \(\tens{V}\) which allows to obtain the same final configuration.
