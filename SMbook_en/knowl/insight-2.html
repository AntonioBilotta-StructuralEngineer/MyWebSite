Insight 1.8.5. meaning of the components of \(\tens{C}\).

The generic component of the tensor \(\tens{C}\) can be expressed as follows

\begin{equation*} C_{ij} = \vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_j\,. \end{equation*}

Consider first the case \(i=j\) regarding the components of the tensor located on the main diagonal. By performing the following manipulations

\begin{equation} C_{ii} = \vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_i = \vec{e}_i \cdot \transp{\tens{F}}\tens{F}\vec{e}_i = \tens{F}\vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{F}\vec{e}_i = \| \tens{F}\vec{e}_i\|^2\,,\tag{1.8.3} \end{equation}

it can be seen that the component \(ii\) on the main diagonal represents the square of the norm of the transformed vector. Since the vector \(\vec{e}_i \) is of unit length, the component \(C_{ii}\) is therefore the square of the stretch along the \(\vec{e}_i\) direction. So what follows can be established

\begin{equation} C_{ii} = \vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_i = \func{\lambda}{\vec{e}_i}^2\,,\tag{1.8.4} \end{equation}

where \(\func{\lambda}{\vec{e}_i}\) indicates the stretch along the \(\vec{e}_i\) direction. Obviously in the case of diagonal shape of the tensor \(\tens{C} \) this stretch coincides with the corresponding principal stretch or eigenvalue of \(\tens{U}\text{.}\)

For the off-diagonal components it can be obtained what follows

\begin{equation*} C_{ij} = \vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_j = \vec{e}_i \cdot \transp{\tens{F}}\tens{F}\vec{e}_j = \tens{F}\vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{F}\vec{e}_j = \cos{\func{\theta}{\tens{F}\vec{e}_i,\tens{F}\vec{e}_j}} \| \tens{F}\vec{e}_i\|\, \| \tens{F}\vec{e}_j\|\,, \end{equation*}

where \(\func{\theta}{\tens{F}\vec{e}_i,\tens{F}\vec{e}_j}\) is the angle between the directions \(\vec{e}_i\) and \(\vec{e}_j\) after the trasnformation. The latter relationship, by using (1.8.3), becomes

\begin{equation*} C_{ij} = \cos{\func{\theta}{\tens{F}\vec{e}_i,\tens{F}\vec{e}_j}} \sqrt{\vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_i} \, \sqrt{\vec{e}_j \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_j}\,. \end{equation*}

Then the generic component \(C_{ij}\) depends, as well as on the stretches along the \(\vec{e}_i\) and \(\vec{e}_j\) directions, on the angle formed by the transforms of these vectors. By introducing the angle

\begin{equation} \func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j} = \func{\Theta}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j} - \func{\theta}{\tens{F}\vec{e}_i,\tens{F}\vec{e}_j} = \frac{\pi}{2} - \func{\theta}{\tens{F}\vec{e}_i,\tens{F}\vec{e}_j}\,,\tag{1.8.5} \end{equation}

which measures the difference between the angles formed by \(\vec{e}_i\) and \(\vec{e}_j\) before and after the transformation, the off-diagonal component can be expressed as

\begin{align*} C_{ij} \amp = \cos{\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j}\right)} \sqrt{\vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_i} \, \sqrt{\vec{e}_j \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_j}\,,\\ \amp = \sin{\func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j}} \sqrt{\vec{e}_i \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_i} \, \sqrt{\vec{e}_j \cdot \tens{C}\vec{e}_j}\,, \end{align*}

which, using la (1.8.4), can be rewritten as

\begin{equation} C_{ij} = \sin{\func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j}}\, \func{\lambda}{\vec{e}_i} \, \func{\lambda}{\vec{e}_j}\,.\tag{1.8.6} \end{equation}

The quantity \(\func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j}\) is called shear angle or shear. When \(\func{\gamma}{\vec{e}_i,\vec{e}_j}\) is zero, i.e. when the angle between \(\vec{e}_i\) and \(\vec{e}_j\) directions does not change, the \(C_{ij}\) component is zero.
